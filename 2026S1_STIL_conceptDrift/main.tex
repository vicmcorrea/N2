\begin{abstract}
We compare three families of diachronic drift signals in Brazilian Portuguese
political discourse: a lexical-profile baseline based on TF-IDF, a static
embedding pipeline based on slice-specific Word2Vec models with Orthogonal
Procrustes alignment, and a contextual analysis based on a Portuguese BERT
checkpoint. The
study uses the floor subset of BrPoliCorpus, covering 24 yearly slices from
2000 to 2023 with 428{,}366 speeches and 63.0 million retained tokens after
preprocessing. To make the methods directly comparable, we build a shared panel
of 55 lemmas containing method-specific drift candidates, stable controls, and
theory seeds. The resulting comparison shows strong disagreement between the
lighter baselines, with Spearman correlation of $-0.54$ and zero overlap in their
top 15 terms, while contextual BERT remains moderately closer to Word2Vec than
to TF-IDF. A filtered contextual panel reduces stable-control leakage and
highlights terms such as \textit{bloqueio}, \textit{sal\'ario},
\textit{interven\c{c}\~ao}, and \textit{elei\c{c}\~ao}. Rather than treating
one detector as externally validated ground truth, we frame the study as an
exploratory comparison of complementary drift sensitivities, interpretability,
and computational cost in Brazilian Portuguese political discourse.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Political discourse is a productive setting for diachronic NLP because shifts in
agenda, coalition structure, institutional conflict, and policy framing leave
traces in lexical choice and word usage over time. Recent work has shown that
distributional models can capture semantic drift at scale, but it has also made
clear that different modeling choices emphasize different aspects of change
\cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic,kutuzov-etal-2018-diachronic}. This is
especially relevant in political corpora, where a term may change because of a
new policy referent, a rhetorical reframing, a shift in collocational profile,
or a reorganization of discourse coalitions rather than because of a neatly
isolated lexical sense change.

That ambiguity matters for evaluation. Shared-task benchmarks have improved
comparability in lexical semantic change detection, but curated external labels
remain scarce, particularly outside the few languages and corpora covered by
standard tasks \cite{schlechtweg-etal-2020-semeval}. At the same time,
distributional pipelines can react to temporal artifacts, alignment effects, and
other confounds if their outputs are interpreted too literally
\cite{dubossarsky-etal-2019-timeout}. For our STIL paper, this means that the
central question is not which detector is ``correct'' in an absolute sense, but
what each detector appears to capture when applied to the same Portuguese
political timeline.

This question is timely for Brazilian Portuguese resources. BrPoliCorpus now
offers a large political corpus spanning multiple institutional genres, while
Roda Viva provides a complementary interview corpus with cleaner speaker turns
and different discourse dynamics
\cite{lima-lopes-2025-brpolicorpus,de-miranda-jr-etal-2024-roda}. We focus here
on the floor subset of BrPoliCorpus as the main discovery corpus and compare a
TF-IDF lexical-profile baseline, a slice-specific aligned Word2Vec baseline, and
a contextual BERT stage scored on a shared comparison panel. The paper asks
three concrete questions: how strongly do the methods agree on the same lemmas,
which candidates remain method-specific, and whether the higher-cost contextual
stage adds enough interpretive value to justify its cost.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

Distributional semantic change detection has evolved from co-occurrence-profile
comparisons to aligned embedding spaces and more recent contextual
representations \cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic,kutuzov-etal-2018-diachronic}.
Aligned static embeddings remain attractive because they are comparatively
efficient and produce interpretable neighborhood movements, but they also
compress all usages of a word into a single representation. This can blur cases
in which older and newer usages coexist rather than replacing one another
cleanly.

Evaluation is the harder problem. The field has benefited from standardized
shared tasks and annotation protocols for lexical semantic change
\cite{schlechtweg-etal-2020-semeval}, yet many real-world studies still operate
without external semantic ground truth. In such settings, negative controls,
corpus-sensitive diagnostics, and cautious interpretation are essential because
apparent change can emerge from temporal referencing, alignment noise, or shifts
in genre and discourse structure rather than from semantic drift alone
\cite{dubossarsky-etal-2019-timeout}. This concern is central to our paper and
directly motivates the use of stable controls and agreement analysis.

Contextual models offer a partial response to the aggregation problem. By
encoding token-level usages, BERT-style representations can surface variation
that static spaces smooth over, and prior work has shown that contextualized
representations can support meaningful lexical semantic change analysis when
paired with usage-sensitive comparison procedures
\cite{giulianelli-etal-2020-analysing,devlin-etal-2019-bert}. Our paper does not
position contextual BERT as a final arbiter. Instead, we place it beside
TF-IDF and aligned Word2Vec on the same Portuguese political panel so that
agreement, disagreement, interpretability, and computational tradeoffs can be
examined directly.

\section{Corpus and Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:data_setup}

The experiments use the floor subset of BrPoliCorpus as the main diachronic
panel. The frozen baseline contains 24 yearly slices from 2000 to 2023, 428{,}366
prepared speeches, and 63{,}036{,}642 retained tokens after normalization,
content-token filtering, and lemma repair. The prepared artifacts preserve
multiple text views, but the main comparative pipeline operates on the
\texttt{content\_lemma} representation. Figure~\ref{fig:corpus_profile} shows the
yearly profile of the corpus. Document volume is high throughout the panel, but
token volume and vocabulary size vary enough to motivate explicit controls for
frequency, slice presence, and candidate eligibility.

We deliberately do not merge BrPoliCorpus and Roda Viva into a single
time-aligned corpus. The present paper uses BrPoliCorpus floor as the sole
discovery panel, while Roda Viva remains a complementary corpus for later
triangulation under separate preprocessing and temporal assumptions.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

The comparison begins with a frozen Word2Vec baseline built from yearly slices,
skip-gram training, and Orthogonal Procrustes alignment across time, following
the standard family of aligned diachronic embedding approaches
\cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic}. We then add a lexical baseline based on
TF-IDF change between adjacent slices and a contextual stage that embeds sampled
in-slice occurrences with the Portuguese checkpoint
\texttt{rufimelo/bert-large-portuguese-cased-sts}. The contextual analysis
extracts layers $-1$ and $-4$; the agreement layer keeps $-1$ as the preferred
paper-facing ranking and uses the second layer as a robustness view.

Rather than compare unrestricted vocabularies, we construct a shared panel of 55
lemmas containing 15 Word2Vec-led drift candidates, 15 TF-IDF-led drift
candidates, 20 stable controls, and 5 theory seeds. This design keeps the
comparison method-neutral: all three families observe the same candidate set,
and disagreement can be interpreted as a property of the signal rather than as
an artifact of divergent vocabularies. The post-run agreement layer then derives
pairwise rank correlations, top-$k$ overlaps, stable-control leakage
diagnostics, and a filtered contextual ranking. Figure~\ref{fig:method_agreement}
visualizes the pairwise agreement structure, while
Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_rank_stats} summarizes overlap and rank-distribution
statistics.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The first result is that the two lighter baselines disagree strongly even after they
are constrained to the same candidate universe. On the shared panel, Word2Vec
and TF-IDF reach Spearman correlation of $-0.54$ and share zero terms in their
top 15 rankings. This inverse association is statistically clear
($p = 2.1 \times 10^{-5}$), which makes the disagreement hard to dismiss as a
small-sample accident. The preferred contextual layer partly mediates this split
without collapsing onto either baseline. Its correlation with Word2Vec is
$0.21$ ($p = 0.128$), while its correlation with TF-IDF is $0.12$
($p = 0.365$). These positive associations are directionally informative but not
strong enough to justify treating contextual BERT as a substitute for one of the
cheaper methods. Figure~\ref{fig:method_agreement} shows the resulting geometry:
the contextual top ranks borrow support from both camps, while the two cheap
methods remain sharply separated.

The second result is that overlap statistics and bucket-level rank distributions
are more informative than raw top-$k$ lists alone. In the preferred BERT layer,
the top-15 overlap is 7 terms with Word2Vec and 6 terms with TF-IDF, whereas the
baseline-to-baseline overlap remains zero. Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_rank_stats} also
shows that selected drift terms are ranked significantly above stable controls by
Word2Vec and TF-IDF ($p < .001$ in both cases), while contextual BERT still
separates the groups but with weaker margins ($p = .008$). This pattern matches
the filtered contextual panel: BERT provides useful adjudication, but the raw
contextual ranking still leaks some stable controls and should not be treated as
an unrestricted final list. The two contextual extraction layers nevertheless
agree strongly with each other (Spearman $= 0.858$), so the broad comparative
picture does not depend on a single extraction depth.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The third result is qualitative rather than purely statistical. Representative
term trajectories show that the methods often disagree about when a term moves,
not only whether it moves at all. Figure~\ref{fig:representative_trajectories}
illustrates four cases: a Word2Vec-led term with strong contextual support
(\textit{bloqueio}), a TF-IDF-led term with strong contextual support
(\textit{sal\'ario}), a theory seed whose contextual rank rises despite modest
static drift (\textit{reforma}), and a stable-control diagnostic term
(\textit{trabalho}) that reveals where contextual leakage remains. These cases
show why a comparative analysis is more informative than a single-method ranking.

The disagreement itself is substantively informative. TF-IDF appears most
sensitive to reweightings in lexical profile: when a term becomes newly central
to yearly agendas, policy disputes, or formulaic framing, its salience can move
even if its local semantic neighborhood remains comparatively stable. Word2Vec,
by contrast, is better positioned to highlight persistent neighborhood
reorganization across slices. Contextual BERT sits between these views. Because
it observes token-level usages, it can support either a TF-IDF-led or a
Word2Vec-led candidate, but the stable-control leakage table shows that greater
context sensitivity does not automatically produce a cleaner unrestricted ranking.

This interpretation fits the term-level evidence. \textit{Sal\'ario} behaves like
a lexical-profile case with strong contextual support, suggesting that changes in
agenda prominence and usage framing are both at work. \textit{Bloqueio} looks
more like a neighborhood-displacement case, where the contextual layer aligns
with the embedding-led signal. \textit{Reforma} is useful for a different
reason: it is not a top filtered contextual term, but as a theory seed it shows
that politically central vocabulary can display contextual differentiation even
when it is not the strongest candidate in the cheap rankings. That is precisely
why the shared panel includes theory seeds and stable controls alongside
method-led drift terms.

The computational comparison in Table~\ref{tab:method_profile} further sharpens
the story. TF-IDF is nearly instantaneous on the frozen run, Word2Vec is still
practical at corpus scale, and contextual BERT is markedly more expensive even
after being restricted to the shared panel. Given the modest rank correlations,
the current evidence does not support replacing the cheaper methods with BERT.
Instead, it supports using contextual modeling selectively as an adjudication and
interpretation layer when disagreement is substantively interesting.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The frozen baseline supports an exploratory comparative study rather than an
argument for a single best detector of semantic change. TF-IDF and Word2Vec
highlight clearly different regions of the candidate space, and contextual BERT
adds a useful but higher-cost inspection layer that remains distinct from both.
For this Portuguese political setting, disagreement is therefore part of the
result: it suggests that the three families are sensitive to different mixtures
of lexical salience, neighborhood displacement, and usage-level variation.

The main limitations follow directly from that framing. First, the paper does
not claim external semantic ground truth for BrPoliCorpus floor. Second, the raw
contextual ranking still requires filtering because stable controls can leak into
the top list. Third, the promising \textit{PTPARL-V} resource is not used here
as validation ground truth because its intervention-level labels still require
explicit deduplication and aggregation rules before they can function as a
separate noisy supervision layer. A natural next step is therefore to test the
current comparison against cleaned \textit{PTPARL-V} supervision while adding a
symbolic companion layer, for instance selected NILC-Metrix-style features, to
distinguish lexical-semantic drift from rhetorical, stylistic, or discourse
organizational change.
