\begin{abstract}
We compare three families of diachronic drift signals in Brazilian Portuguese
political discourse: a lexical-profile baseline based on TF-IDF, a static
embedding pipeline based on slice-specific Word2Vec models with Orthogonal
Procrustes alignment, and a contextual analysis based on Portuguese BERT. The
study uses the floor subset of BrPoliCorpus, covering 24 yearly slices from
2000 to 2023 with 428{,}366 speeches and 63.1 million retained tokens after
preprocessing. To make the methods directly comparable, we build a shared panel
of 55 lemmas containing method-specific drift candidates, stable controls, and
theory seeds. The resulting comparison shows strong disagreement between the
cheap methods, with Spearman correlation of $-0.54$ and zero overlap in their
top 15 terms, while contextual BERT remains moderately closer to Word2Vec than
to TF-IDF. A filtered contextual panel reduces stable-control leakage and
highlights terms such as \textit{bloqueio}, \textit{sal\'ario},
\textit{interven\c{c}\~ao}, and \textit{reforma}. The paper contributes a
paper-ready comparative analysis package for Portuguese political diachronic NLP
and a figure set that makes agreement, disagreement, and term-level trajectories
directly inspectable.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Political discourse is a useful setting for diachronic NLP because lexical
choices, evaluative language, and policy framing shift as coalitions,
administrations, and crises change over time. At the same time, there is no
reason to expect all drift detectors to capture the same phenomenon. Profile
methods such as TF-IDF are sensitive to shifting lexical environments, static
embedding pipelines emphasize local neighborhood movement after alignment, and
contextual encoders may privilege usage-level distinctions that are invisible to
lighter approaches. This paper therefore treats method comparison itself as the
core research object. We ask how strongly the methods agree on a shared set of
Portuguese political terms, which candidates remain method-specific, and whether
the expensive contextual layer contributes information beyond cheaper baselines.

\section{Corpus and Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:data_setup}

The experiments use the floor subset of BrPoliCorpus as the main diachronic
panel. The frozen baseline contains 24 yearly slices from 2000 to 2023, 428{,}366
prepared speeches, and 63{,}071{,}705 retained tokens after normalization,
content-token filtering, and lemma repair. The prepared artifacts preserve
multiple text views, but the main comparative pipeline operates on the
\texttt{content\_lemma} representation. Figure~\ref{fig:corpus_profile} shows the
yearly profile of the corpus. Document volume is high throughout the panel, but
token volume and vocabulary size vary enough to motivate explicit controls for
frequency, slice presence, and candidate eligibility.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

The comparison begins with a frozen Word2Vec baseline built from yearly slices,
skip-gram training, and Orthogonal Procrustes alignment across time. We then add
a lexical baseline based on TF-IDF change between adjacent slices and a
contextual analysis based on Portuguese BERT over sampled in-slice occurrences.
Rather than compare unrestricted vocabularies, we construct a shared panel of 55
lemmas containing 15 Word2Vec-led drift candidates, 15 TF-IDF-led drift
candidates, 20 stable controls, and 5 theory seeds. The contextual stage scores
that shared panel and the post-run agreement layer derives pairwise rank
correlations, top-$k$ overlaps, stable-control leakage diagnostics, and a
filtered contextual ranking. Figure~\ref{fig:method_agreement} visualizes the
pairwise agreement structure, while Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_rank_stats}
summarizes overlap and rank-distribution statistics.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

The first result is that the two cheap methods disagree strongly even after they
are constrained to the same candidate universe. On the shared panel, Word2Vec
and TF-IDF reach Spearman correlation of $-0.54$ and share zero terms in their
top 15 rankings. The preferred contextual layer partly mediates this split, but
it does not collapse onto either baseline. Its correlation with Word2Vec is
$0.21$, while its correlation with TF-IDF is $0.12$. Figure~\ref{fig:method_agreement}
shows that the contextual top ranks retain clear support for both method camps.

The second result is that overlap statistics and bucket-level rank distributions
are more informative than raw top-$k$ lists alone. In the preferred BERT layer,
the top-15 overlap is 7 terms with Word2Vec and 6 terms with TF-IDF, whereas the
cheap-method overlap remains zero. Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_rank_stats} also
shows that selected drift terms are ranked significantly above stable controls by
Word2Vec and TF-IDF ($p < .001$ in both cases), while contextual BERT still
separates the groups but with weaker margins ($p = .008$). This pattern matches
the filtered contextual panel: BERT provides useful adjudication, but the raw
contextual ranking still leaks some stable controls and should not be treated as
an unrestricted final list.

The third result is qualitative rather than purely statistical. Representative
term trajectories show that the methods often disagree about when a term moves,
not only whether it moves at all. Figure~\ref{fig:representative_trajectories}
illustrates four cases: a Word2Vec-led term with strong contextual support
(\textit{bloqueio}), a TF-IDF-led term with strong contextual support
(\textit{sal\'ario}), a theory seed whose contextual rank rises despite modest
static drift (\textit{reforma}), and a stable-control diagnostic term
(\textit{trabalho}) that reveals where contextual leakage remains. These cases
show why a comparative analysis is more informative than a single-method ranking.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The current frozen baseline supports an exploratory comparative paper rather than
an argument for a single best drift detector. Word2Vec and TF-IDF highlight
different regions of the candidate space, while contextual BERT adds a useful
adjudication layer that is closer to Word2Vec than to TF-IDF but still distinct
from both. The main remaining limitation is that the contextual stage still
requires filtering to suppress stable-control leakage. The next steps are to add
qualitative agreement packets, finalize the literature-grounded framing, and
convert the current figure package into the camera-ready STIL submission.
