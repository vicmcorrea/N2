\begin{abstract}
The shift in political discourse often reflects deeper societal changes, but quantifying how specific terms evolve semantically over decades remains a significant challenge for computational linguistics. To systematically compare existing approaches under different computational constraints, we propose a cost-aware, model- and language-agnostic comparative framework for lexical semantic change detection. 
% We use it to compare three families of diachronic drift signals in Brazilian Portuguese political discourse, namely a lexical-profile baseline based on TF-IDF, a static embedding pipeline based on slice-specific Word2Vec models with Orthogonal Procrustes alignment, and a contextual analysis based on a Portuguese BERT checkpoint.
We apply it to Brazilian Portuguese political discourse using a shared evaluation panel of 55 lemmas combining drift candidates, stable controls, and theory-driven seeds. We compare three families of diachronic drift signals: TF-IDF lexical profiles, slice-specific Word2Vec models with Orthogonal Procrustes alignment, and a contextual BERT-based approach.
%
The results shows strong disagreement between the
lighter baselines, with Spearman correlation of $-0.54$ and no overlap in their
top-ranked terms, while contextual BERT remains moderately closer to Word2Vec than
to TF-IDF. A filtered contextual panel reduces stable-control leakage and
highlights terms such as ``\textit{bloqueio}'', ``\textit{sal\'ario}'',
``\textit{interven\c{c}\~ao}'', and ``\textit{elei\c{c}\~ao}''. Rather than treating
one detector as externally validated ground truth, this study presents an
exploratory comparison of complementary drift sensitivities, interpretability,
and computational cost in Brazilian Portuguese political discourse. %The framework is model- and language-agnostic, offering a reusable blueprint for multi-method drift studies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Political discourse is a productive setting for diachronic NLP because shifts in agenda, coalition structure, and policy framing leave traces in lexical choice and word usage over time. 
The language of politics reflects historical change, as shifts in ideological agendas, coalition structures, and policy framing become observable in patterns of lexical choice and word usage over time. However, manually analyzing large-scale and evolving patterns in political discourse is challenging. Diachronic natural language processing (NLP) methods offer promising avenues for analysis. Among these, distributional models can capture some of these movements at scale, but different modeling choices foreground different aspects
of change
\cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic,kutuzov-etal-2018-diachronic,tahmasebi-etal-2021-computational}.
In political corpora the situation is especially complex, since a term may drift
because of a new policy referent, a rhetorical reframing, a shift in
collocational profile, or a reorganization of discourse coalitions. None of
these mechanisms reduces neatly to a single lexical sense change. 

This complexity carries over to evaluation, where shared-task benchmarks have
improved comparability in lexical semantic change
detection~\cite{schlechtweg-etal-2020-semeval} but curated external labels
remain scarce, especially outside the few languages and corpora covered by
standard tasks. Distributional pipelines can also react to temporal artifacts,
alignment noise, and other confounds when outputs are read too
literally~\cite{dubossarsky-etal-2019-timeout}. Without externally validated
ground truth, the central question becomes not which detector is ``correct''
but what each detector appears to capture when applied to the same political
timeline.

This question is timely for Brazilian Portuguese. Large-scale political corpora
such as BrPoliCorpus \cite{lima-lopes-2025-brpolicorpus} are only now becoming
available, yet comparative drift studies that place multiple detection families
side by side on the same Portuguese political material remain rare. % To structure this comparison, we introduce a cost-aware comparative framework that stages detection methods by computational expense and channels their outputs through a shared comparison panel for cross-method agreement analysis. We instantiate the framework on the floor subset of BrPoliCorpus, which covers 24 yearly slices of Chamber speeches from 2000 to 2023, comparing a TF-IDF lexical-profile baseline, a slice-specific aligned Word2Vec pipeline, and a contextual BERT stage, all scored on a shared comparison panel of 55 lemmas.
To address this gap, we propose a cost-aware comparative framework for diachronic semantic change analysis that stages drift detection methods into three computational tiers of increasing complexity: lexical-statistical, static-embedding, and contextual representations. This design enables controlled comparison of heterogeneous signals through a shared evaluation panel. 

We instantiate this framework on the floor subset of BrPoliCorpus, covering 24 yearly slices of Brazilian Chamber of Deputies speeches from 2000 to 2023. For the lexical-statistical tier of the framework, we use a TF-IDF lexical-profile baseline; for the static-embedding tier, a slice-specific Word2Vec pipeline with Orthogonal Procrustes alignment; and for the contextual tier, a BERT-based model. All three approaches are evaluated on a shared panel of 55 lemmas.

The paper addresses the following questions: 

\begin{enumerate}
    \item How strongly do the drift detection methods agree on the same lemmas? 

    \item Which lemmas identified as drift candidates are method-specific?

    \item Does the higher-cost contextual stage of the proposed framework add enough interpretive value to justify its computational expense?
\end{enumerate}

Beyond addressing these questions for Portuguese political discourse, where
published multi-method drift analyses remain much scarcer than in the
better-studied English-centered literature, this work contributes a reusable
framework design that can be instantiated with alternative models, corpora, and
languages.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

Distributional semantic change detection has evolved from co-occurrence
comparisons to aligned static embeddings and then to contextual
representations~\cite{kutuzov-etal-2018-diachronic,tahmasebi-etal-2021-computational}.
Among static approaches, slice-specific Word2Vec models aligned with Orthogonal
Procrustes have been shown to recover known historical shifts and reveal
statistical regularities of
change~\cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic}.
Such embeddings remain attractive because they are efficient and produce
interpretable neighborhood movements, but they compress all usages of a word
into a single vector, blurring cases in which older and newer usages coexist.

Despite these methodological advances, evaluation remains a central challenge. Standardized shared tasks have improved
comparability \cite{schlechtweg-etal-2020-semeval}, yet many real-world studies
still operate without external semantic ground truth, and apparent change can
emerge from temporal referencing, alignment noise, or genre shifts rather than
from semantic drift alone \cite{dubossarsky-etal-2019-timeout}. These concerns
motivates the stable controls and agreement analysis in the present work.

%Contextual models offer a partial response to the aggregation problem. BERT-style representations encode token-level usages and can surface variation that static spaces smooth over \cite{giulianelli-etal-2020-analysing,devlin-etal-2019-bert}.
Contextual models provide a complementary perspective. BERT-style representations encode token-level usages and can surface variation that static spaces smooth over \cite{giulianelli-etal-2020-analysing,devlin-etal-2019-bert}.
Scalable contextual methods that improve interpretability have also been
proposed \cite{montariol-etal-2021-scalable}, and pre-trained Portuguese
checkpoints such as BERTimbau \cite{souza-etal-2020-bertimbau} have made
contextual analysis feasible for Portuguese NLP\@. % We place contextual BERT beside TF-IDF and aligned Word2Vec on the same political panel so that agreement, disagreement, and computational tradeoffs can be examined directly.

Diachronic analysis of political language has gained traction through
parliamentary corpora. The study of \cite{hofmann-etal-2020-comparing}
compared lexical usage in Austrian political discourse across time, but
comparable multi-method studies on Brazilian Portuguese political speech remain
scarce. Existing Portuguese diachronic
resources \cite{osorio-cardoso-2025-historical} have focused primarily on
historical and literary material rather than political discourse, leaving a
gap that BrPoliCorpus \cite{lima-lopes-2025-brpolicorpus} now fills with the
scale needed for controlled comparative work.

Prior work has typically examined lexical-statistical, static-embedding, and contextual approaches separately, with limited direct comparison on the same political timeline. To address this gap and extend comparative diachronic analysis to Brazilian Portuguese political discourse, this study introduces a comparative framework for jointly evaluating these families of methods under shared data and evaluation conditions.

\section{Comparative Drift Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

This section presents the general, model-agnostic design of the proposed framework. The source code of the framework and the experiments is publicly available
at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/STIL2025 conceptdrift-3DAC}. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} then details how it is instantiated with specific models and configurations. The framework proposed in this study is summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:study_design}. 

\begin{figure}[!htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.90\linewidth, trim=0.2cm 0.2cm 0.2cm 0.2cm, clip]{figs/paper/figure_05_study_design_v2.pdf}
    \caption{Staged comparative framework for lexical semantic change
    detection. Two cheaper tiers screen the scored vocabulary and nominate
    drift candidates; a shared comparison panel combines those candidates
    with stable controls and theory seeds before the expensive contextual
    tier and agreement analysis.}
    \label{fig:study_design}
\end{figure}

The framework assumes a diachronic corpus partitioned into temporal slices,
where each slice must contain enough documents and tokens to support stable
frequency estimates and distributional model training. A preprocessing
step then normalizes surface forms to a shared lemma representation so that all
three tiers operate on the same vocabulary. Not every word form is suitable
for drift scoring, so the framework applies three joint filters before any
method runs: a minimum per-slice frequency to guarantee sufficient token mass
for embedding training, a document-dispersion threshold to prevent a single
source from inflating apparent salience, and a minimum slice-presence rate to
restrict attention to genuinely diachronic terms rather than episodic ones.
Drift and stable-control candidates may be further restricted to dominant
part-of-speech classes.

The framework then organizes lexical semantic change detection into three
computational tiers arranged by increasing computational cost, each targeting a distinct
facet of diachronic drift. The first tier, a \textbf{lexical-statistical
baseline}, operates on surface-level frequency or weighting counts and
requires no model training, capturing shifts in lexical salience at negligible
computational expense. The second tier, a \textbf{static-embedding method},
maps each word type to a single dense vector per temporal slice and aligns
slice-specific models into a common geometric space so that drift becomes a
measurable displacement; training cost is moderate and results are
interpretable through nearest-neighbor inspection. The third tier, a
\textbf{contextual-embedding method}, relies on a pretrained language model
that produces a distinct vector for every token occurrence, which means this
tier can in principle detect sense-level divergence that the cheaper stages
miss, though it is the most expensive and the hardest to interpret.

The key design principle is \emph{staged cost awareness}. %The two cheaper tiers screen the full scored vocabulary first, and their method-led candidates feed forward into the expensive contextual stage, which avoids running heavy inference over every eligible lemma. Cross-method comparison then requires a fixed evaluation set that prevents each method from being assessed only on the terms it would naturally favour. 
The two cheaper tiers first score the full eligible vocabulary for diachronic drift, using
their own representations to estimate how strongly each lemma changes across
temporal slices. Their highest-ranked candidates then feed forward into the expensive contextual stage, which avoids running heavy inference over every eligible lemma. Cross-method comparison in turn requires a fixed evaluation set that prevents each method from being assessed only on the terms it would naturally favour. 
The framework therefore prescribes a
\textbf{shared comparison panel} assembled from four sources: (i)~high-drift
candidates nominated by the lexical-statistical baseline, (ii)~high-drift candidates nominated by the static-embedding method, 
%(iii)~low-drift stable controls selected jointly by both cheaper tiers, 
(iii)~low-drift stable controls (lemmas ranked lowest by both cheaper methods),
and (iv)~theory-driven seed
terms drawn from domain expertise or prior work. Because all three tiers score
exactly the same candidate set, any disagreement reflects genuine differences
in what each method measures rather than divergent vocabularies.

%On the shared panel, the framework computes an \emph{agreement analysis layer} consisting of pairwise rank correlations, top-$k$ overlap curves, and bucket-level rank distributions that separate drift candidates from stable controls. These diagnostics reveal where the tiers converge, where they diverge, and whether the more expensive contextual stage adds enough interpretive value to justify its cost; the analysis layer itself is agnostic to the specific models plugged into each tier.
Finally, the last component of the proposed framework is the \textbf{agreement analysis layer}, which evaluates the rankings produced when the candidate lemmas in the shared comparison panel are scored by the different tiers, including the contextual stage. First, rank-correlation measures quantify how similarly pairs of methods order the same panel by drift. Second, top-$k$ overlap curves show how many lemma candidates are shared when progressively larger top-ranked sets are compared. Third, stable-control leakage rates track whether lemmas designated as low-drift controls are nevertheless placed near the top of a method's ranking. Fourth, a filtered contextual ranking removes such leaked controls to support qualitative inspection of contextual candidates.

In this study, in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}, we present a particular instantiation of the proposed framework detailed in Figure \ref{fig:study_design}. However, the
framework is model- and language-agnostic, and any combination of
methods that respects the three-tier cost ordering can be substituted.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

We instantiate the comparative framework of Section~\ref{sec:framework} on
Brazilian Portuguese parliamentary discourse. 

\subsection{Corpus and Preprocessing}

A growing number of diachronic Portuguese resources are now available, as documented by \cite{osorio-cardoso-2025-historical}, ranging from syntactically annotated literary collections such as the Tycho Brahe Parsed Corpus~\cite{galves-faria-2017-tycho} to large-scale multi-genre repositories such as the Corpus do Portugu\^es~\cite{davies-ferreira-2006-corpusdoportugues}. However, these resources
target historical-linguistic research across broad periods rather than
domain-specific political analysis with continuous yearly slices. BrPoliCorpus \cite{lima-lopes-2025-brpolicorpus} helps address this gap
by providing large-scale parliamentary data for Brazilian Portuguese.

The experiments therefore use its subset containing  Brazilian Chamber floor
speeches from 2000 to 2023 organized into 24 yearly slices. Within
BrPoliCorpus, the inaugural-speech subset reaches back to 1889 but
contains only 35 documents, too few for stable distributional estimation. The
floor subset offers the largest contiguous yearly coverage (428{,}366 speeches
across 24 slices), a uniform parliamentary register, and exact date metadata,
making it the most suitable segment for a year-by-year comparative drift study
in the political domain.

As described in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, not all tokens are eligible for drift scoring. Raw speeches are tokenized and tagged with
\texttt{spaCy} (\texttt{pt\_core\_news\_sm}). We retain nouns, verbs, and
adjectives, exclude proper nouns, remove stopwords, punctuation, and numeric
tokens, and keep only tokens with at least two characters. All text is
lowercased with accents preserved. Each token is then mapped to its lemma form,
producing the \texttt{content\_lemma} representation used by all three tiers of the proposed framework (Figure \ref{fig:study_design}).
After preprocessing, the corpus retains 428{,}366 speeches and
63{,}036{,}642 tokens across 225{,}484 unique lemmas.
Table~\ref{tab:dataset_summary} summarizes the corpus totals and the quartile
structure of yearly variation.

A lemma enters the scored vocabulary only if it appears at least 50 times per
slice, in at least 5 documents per slice, and in at least 80\% of the 24
slices. These thresholds were fixed as practical corpus-stability defaults
rather than tuned to optimize any particular method, and may vary when applied to corpora with different size, genre composition, or temporal density. 

The frequency floor
ensures sufficient token mass for reliable embedding training in each yearly
slice; the document-dispersion requirement prevents a single speaker or
parliamentary session from inflating a lemma's apparent salience; and the
slice-presence constraint restricts attention to lemmas that are genuinely
diachronic rather than episodic. Drift and stable-control candidates are
further restricted to nouns and adjectives, and a small set of procedural
high-frequency terms is excluded. These filters reduce the scored vocabulary
to 3{,}221 lemmas for the two cheaper methods.

Yearly document counts range from 3{,}041 to 26{,}868 (median 16{,}752) and
retained token volume from 630K to 4.2M (median 2.6M). Document volume is high
throughout, but token and vocabulary variation across slices motivates the
frequency and slice-presence controls described above.

\subsection{Drift detection tiers}

Each tier of the proposed framework (Section \ref{sec:framework}) is instantiated with a specific, well-established model: TF-IDF for the lexical-statistical baseline,
slice-aligned Word2Vec with Orthogonal Procrustes for the static-embedding
tier, and BERTimbau Large for the contextual tier. The individual techniques
are grounded in the literature~\cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic,giulianelli-etal-2020-analysing} %; our contribution is their orchestration through the staged pipeline and shared panel design.
and are integrated through the staged pipeline and shared panel design introduced in Section~\ref{sec:framework}.

% \subsubsection{TF-IDF Baseline}

% For each eligible lemma we compute a TF-IDF vector
% across yearly slices using smoothed inverse-document frequency. Drift is

For the \textbf{lexical-statistical tier}, we compute a \textbf{TF-IDF} vector for each eligible lemma across yearly slices using smoothed inverse-document frequency. Drift is measured as the mean absolute change in TF-IDF weight between adjacent slices. This captures shifts in lexical salience, reflecting how central a term becomes to each year's agenda, without relying on distributional neighborhoods.

% \subsubsection{Word2Vec Baseline}

% We train independent skip-gram models per yearly
% slice (300 dimensions, window~5, 5 negative samples, minimum count~5, 5 epochs,
% 2 replicates with different seeds) and align them into a common space with
% Orthogonal Procrustes over the top 20{,}000 anchor words
% \cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic}. Drift is the mean cosine distance between
% a lemma's aligned vectors in consecutive slices, averaged over replicates. This
% captures persistent neighborhood reorganization.

For the \textbf{static-embedding tier}, we train independent \textbf{Word2Vec} (skip-gram) models per yearly slice (300 dimensions, window~5, 5 negative samples, minimum count~5, 5 epochs, 2 replicates with different seeds) and align them into a common space using Orthogonal Procrustes over the top 20,000 anchor words~\cite{hamilton-etal-2016-diachronic}. Drift is computed as the mean cosine distance between a lemma's aligned vectors in consecutive slices, averaged over replicates. This captures persistent neighborhood reorganization.

% \subsubsection{Contextual BERT}

% We embed sampled in-context occurrences with a Portuguese
% BERT checkpoint (BERTimbau Large~\cite{souza-etal-2020-bertimbau}),
% extracting hidden-state layers $-1$ and~$-4$. For each lemma and slice, up to 64 occurrences are sampled within a
% 48-token context window; the contextual drift score is the mean cosine distance
% between slice-level centroid embeddings in consecutive slices. The agreement
% layer keeps layer~$-1$ as the preferred ranking and uses layer~$-4$ as a

For the \textbf{contextual tier}, we embed sampled in-context occurrences using a Portuguese BERT checkpoint (\textbf{BERTimbau Large}~\cite{souza-etal-2020-bertimbau}), extracting hidden states from layers $-1$ and~$-4$. For each lemma and slice, up to 64 occurrences are sampled within a 48-token context window. The contextual drift score is computed as the mean cosine distance between slice-level centroid embeddings across consecutive slices. The agreement layer uses layer $-1$ as the primary ranking and layer $-4$ as a robustness check.

The two lighter baselines disagree strongly even after they are constrained to
the same candidate universe. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:method_agreement}, the Word2Vec vs.\ TF-IDF panel displays a clear negative trend: lemmas ranked highly by one baseline tend to be ranked low by the other. Quantitatively, the two methods reach Spearman $\rho = -0.54$ ($p = 2.1 \times 10^{-5}$), confirming that they capture substantially different facets of change. %and share zero terms in their top 15 rankings. 
Their top-15 overlap is zero, further showing that TF-IDF and Word2Vec favor markedly different drift candidates because they measure different types of change.

% The BERT vs.\ Word2Vec and BERT vs.\ TF-IDF panels show that contextual BERT draws partial support from both camps without collapsing onto either, while the BERT layer comparison confirms that the two extraction layers agree closely with each other.
%The preferred contextual layer partly mediates this split without collapsing onto either baseline. Overlap statistics and bucket-level rank distributions prove more informative than raw top-$k$ lists alone. In the preferred BERT layer, the top-15 overlap is 7 terms with Word2Vec and 6 terms with TF-IDF, whereas the baseline-to-baseline overlap remains zero. Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_rank_stats} also shows that selected drift terms are ranked significantly above stable controls by Word2Vec and TF-IDF ($p < .001$ in both cases), while contextual BERT still separates the groups but with weaker margins ($p = .008$). This pattern is consistent with the filtered contextual panel, where BERT provides useful adjudication but the raw contextual ranking still leaks some stable controls and should not be treated as an unrestricted final list. The two contextual extraction layers nevertheless agree strongly with each other (Spearman $= 0.858$), so the broad comparative picture does not depend on a single extraction depth. This high inter-layer agreement accords with findings that mid-to-upper layers carry most of the semantic change signal, while the final layer increasingly encodes surface-form information that can bias change scores~\cite{laicher-etal-2021-explaining,cassotti-etal-2024-systematic}; the stability between layers $-1$ and $-4$ in our setting suggests thatorthographic bias does not yet dominate, though it does not rule it out.

Figure~\ref{fig:method_agreement} also shows that the contextual tier partly mediates this split without collapsing onto either baseline. In the BERT vs.\ Word2Vec and BERT vs.\ TF-IDF panels, contextual BERT displays weak but positive associations with both methods. On the preferred layer ($-1$), BERT reaches $\rho = 0.21$ ($p = 0.128$) with Word2Vec and $\rho = 0.12$ ($p = 0.365$) with TF-IDF. These positive associations are directionally informative but not strong enough to justify treating contextual BERT as a substitute for either cheaper method. Instead, the contextual stage acts as an intermediate signal that partially aligns with both methods. The BERT layer comparison further shows that layers $-4$ and $-1$ agree closely (Spearman $\rho = 0.858$), indicating that the contextual conclusions are not driven by a single extraction depth and remain stable across nearby upper layers. This strong inter-layer agreement is consistent with prior findings that mid-to-upper layers carry most of the semantic change signal, while the final layer increasingly encodes surface-form information that can bias change scores~\cite{laicher-etal-2021-explaining,cassotti-etal-2024-systematic}. In our setting, the stability between layers $-1$ and $-4$ suggests that such orthographic bias does not dominate, although it cannot be fully ruled out.

The Word2Vec vs.\ TF-IDF scatter reveals the strong negative trend where terms ranked highly by one baseline tend to be ranked low by the other, confirming that they capture fundamentally different facets of change. The BERT vs.\ Word2Vec and BERT vs.\ TF-IDF panels show that contextual BERT draws partial support from both camps without collapsing onto either, while the BERT layer comparison confirms that the two extraction layers agree closely with each other.

Figure~\ref{fig:overlap_rank_stats} reports the remaining diagnostics of the agreement layer. The top-left panel shows top-$k$ overlap across methods, while the remaining panels compare rank-percentile distributions of drift-candidate buckets and stable controls for Word2Vec, TF-IDF, and BERT.

the top-15 overlap is 7 terms with Word2Vec and 6 terms with TF-IDF, whereas the
baseline-to-baseline overlap remains zero. This pattern is consistent with the filtered contextual panel, where BERT provides useful adjudication but the raw contextual ranking still leaks some stable controls and should not be treated as an unrestricted final list. The two contextual extraction layers nevertheless agree strongly with each other (Spearman $= 0.858$), so the broad comparative picture does not depend on a single extraction depth. This high inter-layer agreement accords with findings that mid-to-upper layers carry most of the semantic change signal, while the final layer increasingly encodes surface-form information that can bias change scores~\cite{laicher-etal-2021-explaining,cassotti-etal-2024-systematic}; the stability between layers $-1$ and $-4$ in our setting suggests that orthographic bias does not yet dominate, though it does not rule it out.
Bucket-level rank distributions further evaluate whether each method separates nominated drift terms from stable controls. Word2Vec and TF-IDF rank drift candidates significantly above stable controls ($p < .001$ in both cases, one-sided Mann--Whitney), showing clean bucket separation. BERT also separates the groups, but with weaker margins ($p = .008$). This weaker contrast is consistent with inspection of the raw contextual ranking, where some stable controls still appear near the top positions.

For that reason, the contextual ranking is most useful as a selective interpretive layer rather than as an unrestricted final candidate list. After filtering leaked controls, the remaining contextual candidates provide informative arbitration among terms proposed by the cheaper methods.

As a robustness check, we recomputed contextual scores using average pairwise
distance (APD)~\cite{giulianelli-etal-2020-analysing} instead of the centroid-based
metric. APD operates on all individual occurrence embeddings rather than on
slice-level centroids, giving equal influence to each usage regardless of
embedding magnitude. On the preferred layer, APD and centroid rankings
correlate at Spearman $\rho = 0.79$ ($p < 10^{-12}$), with 7 of 15 top terms
shared. The two metrics foreground different properties: APD promotes
high-frequency polysemous terms whose embedding clouds are broad (e.g.\
\textit{trabalho}, \textit{p\'ublico}), while the centroid metric favours
terms with coherent directional shifts (\textit{bloqueio},
\textit{sal\'ario}). Notably, the centroid metric separates method-nominated
drift candidates from stable controls more cleanly than APD ($p = .008$ vs.\
$p = .081$, Mann--Whitney). The strong overall correlation confirms that the
comparative conclusions are robust to the choice of contextual aggregation,
while the separation difference suggests that centroid distance is a less
noisy signal for this panel-based comparison.

In summary, the results support the cost-aware logic of the framework. The two cheaper tiers provide distinct candidate pools at low cost, while the more expensive contextual stage adds useful comparative evidence when disagreement between baselines is itself informative.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper introduced a shared, cost-aware comparative framework for contrasting three families of lexical semantic change detectors in Brazilian Portuguese political discourse. The research questions posed in the
introduction receive three main answers from the evidence.

First, regarding the strength of inter-method
agreement, TF-IDF and Word2Vec disagree strongly on the shared panel and contextual BERT remains only weakly
correlated with either baseline, so the three families do not converge on a single ranking.
Second, regarding method-specific candidates, %all drift terms nominated by the two cheaper methods are exclusive to one baseline. 
the two cheaper methods identify largely non-overlapping sets of drift terms, indicating that each baseline highlights different aspects of lexical change.
BERT partially bridges
this split by placing candidates from both camps among its top-ranked terms,
but it also retains some stable controls in high positions, so its raw ranking
should not be treated as an unrestricted candidate list. Third, regarding the
cost--benefit of the contextual stage, BERT provides useful adjudication when
inter-method disagreement is substantively interesting, as illustrated by
terms such as \textit{bloqueio}, \textit{sal\'ario}, and \textit{reforma}.
At roughly $600{\times}$ the cost of TF-IDF, though, the current evidence
favors using it selectively rather than as a replacement for the cheaper
methods. 

Taken together, these results frame disagreement itself as part of the
contribution, suggesting that the three method families are sensitive to
different mixtures of lexical
salience, neighborhood displacement, and usage-level variation in Brazilian
Portuguese political discourse. Because the comparative framework
(Section~\ref{sec:framework}) specifies roles and interfaces rather than
particular models, it can be instantiated with alternative static or
contextual embeddings, applied to other languages, or extended with
additional tiers.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The primary evaluative limitation is that this paper does not claim external semantic ground truth for BrPoliCorpus floor, so agreement and disagreement are analyzed as comparative evidence rather than as proof that one detector is correct. A second, methodological limitation is that the raw contextual ranking still admits some stable controls near the top, even though the filtered contextual list is useful for interpretation; contextual sensitivity does not automatically yield a cleaner unrestricted candidate list. The choice of contextual aggregation strategy also merits discussion. We report centroid-based drift as the primary contextual metric and verify it against APD~\cite{giulianelli-etal-2020-analysing,cassotti-etal-2024-systematic} (Section~\ref{sec:results}); the strong correlation and the centroid metric's tighter bucket separation support this choice, though APD captures complementary embedding-cloud variance. Sense-based clustering methods~\cite{montariol-etal-2021-scalable} were not attempted here. Finally, yearly slicing is appropriate for a first comparative baseline but compresses within-year shocks, campaign cycles, and short-lived institutional disputes that may matter for political language.

Future work could address the evaluative gap through external annotation or noisy supervision from labeled parliamentary datasets, though such resources still require careful deduplication before serving as reliable comparison targets. On the modeling side, adopting task-specialized
models such as XL-LEXEME~\cite{cassotti-etal-2024-systematic}, exploring newer divergence metrics~\cite{rachinskiy-schlechtweg-2025-rethinking}, and applying orthographic-bias mitigation through subword masking~\cite{laicher-etal-2021-explaining} would bring the contextual stage closer to current best practices. A symbolic companion analysis using lexical complexity or readability features could also help separate lexical-semantic drift from rhetorical or stylistic change.

\section{Ethics Statement}
\label{sec:ethics}

The study analyzes public institutional political speech and reports only
aggregate corpus statistics, ranked lemmas, and usage-level comparative signals.
We do not profile individual speakers or infer private attributes. Even so,
political corpora encode unequal representation and occasionally harmful
rhetoric, so drift scores should not be read as normative judgments, evidence
of speaker intent, or standalone claims about social meaning. All outputs are
exploratory comparative signals whose interpretation depends on corpus
composition, preprocessing choices, and the distinction between lexical
salience, contextual variation, and broader discourse change.
